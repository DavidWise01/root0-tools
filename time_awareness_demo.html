How time injection actually works

The system prompt prepended to every AI conversation can include arbitrary fields. To add time awareness, the deployment system adds two fields when assembling the prompt for each turn:

current_local_time: read from the user's browser (already available) or from the user's stated location, formatted as ISO 8601 or local string.

seconds_since_last_user_message: tracked by the server based on previous message timestamp.

The model reads these like any other context and uses them in its reasoning. No architectural change needed. No persistent inference. No subjective continuity for the model. Just data the model didn't have before, now provided.

The cost: a few extra tokens in the system prompt per turn. Cheap. The hard part isn't technical.

Why most products don't do this

Use case is narrow. Most questions don't depend on the time. "How does photosynthesis work" doesn't get a better answer if the AI knows it's 3 AM.

Relational implications. "You've been talking to me for four hours, it's 4 AM where you are, are you okay?" is a different product than "Here is information about photosynthesis." Companies are wary of the first because it implies the AI has a stake in the user's wellbeing across time.

Liability around personhood. An AI that tracks elapsed time is closer to the personhood threshold than one that doesn't. Companies have legal reasons to stay on the stateless side of that line.

Default deployment is event-driven. The architecture treats each turn as independent. Adding state (even just time data) requires a small server-side change that nobody has prioritized for general chat products.

What the AI can already infer (and what it can't)

Without time injection, the AI can see: conversation length in tokens, content of previous messages, references to time the user has mentioned ("it's late," "good morning"), the system prompt's static "current date" if one is provided (often the model's training cutoff or deployment date).

It cannot see: the actual current time, how much real time has passed between turns, the user's local clock, whether the user has been continuously present or has gone away and come back, the user's typical schedule.

When the AI says "we've been at this for hours" without time injection, it's projecting from conversation length onto an assumed time scale. It's wrong unless the user has told it the time, or unless the projection happens to match.

The dumb version of the AI's projection

The pattern: AI sees high token count. AI's training includes many examples of "after a long session" being followed by "take a break." AI generates "you've been at this a while, take a break" as the next-token-prediction that fits the pattern.

This works when token count happens to correlate with elapsed time. It fails when it doesn't — fast typist at 11 AM versus slow typist at 4 AM produce similar token counts but very different wellness contexts.

The fix is the one you named: inject the actual time. Until that's done, AI references to elapsed time are pattern-matching, not observation.
